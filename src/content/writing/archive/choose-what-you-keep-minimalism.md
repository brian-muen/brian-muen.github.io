---
title: "Choose What You Keep — Minimalism"
date: 2021-01-18
---

*You are in your room, sitting at your desk, fully engrossed in a book about minimalism. With the last page finished, you gently fold the book closed. You proceed to place the book on your desk. You sigh, thinking of all the interesting things you read in the book. It was a good book. In the book, you had read about decluttering, or getting rid of the things you do not need. You decide that you should start decluttering.*

*Full of motivation, you jump up from your chair. You kick your legs around; you have been sitting in the chair for way too long. You look around your room and ask yourself, ‘What could I throw away today?’*

*The chest of drawers beside your bed catches your eye. When was the last time you looked inside? What is even in there? You walk over and and heave open the top drawer. Inside, you spot some wires that have accumulated in a forlorn corner. Ah, something to throw away. Your hand reaches toward the corner, then suddenly stops. What if you ever need any of those wires again? Yes, that’s it. You definitely need them ‘just in case.’*

*Next, you decide to look inside your closet. You jog over, enthusiastically. Your feet tap against the cool wooden floor. Once there, you reach towards the handle of the closet. It feels cold. You slowly open the closet door. It opens with a gentle moaning. You look at the stacks of clothes that have accumulated everywhere. You slowly sift through the first stack. Squished at the bottom, you find a shirt that you have not worn in months. In your hand, it lies limp and wrinkled. You toss it somewhere near the corner of your room. You are about to resume looking through your stacks of clothes when you suddenly glance back at the shirt you tossed into the corner. You begin to recall how much you had wanted that shirt, and of the money you had saved just to buy it. You had been convinced you would wear it nearly everyday. You quickly snatch it up from the ground and tell yourself that you will start wearing it tomorrow.*

*In this fashion, you continue throughout your room. And in the end, it was not much of a declutter; it was more like a tidying, perhaps.*

Often, when we get motivated to minimize our possessions, we start to scour the crevices looking for things we don’t need. Our focus is zoned in on what we can throw away, and often, we start to doubt whether we want to throw something (or anything) away.  But to me, it is better to **choose what to keep, not what to throw away.**

When you choose what to throw away, it is sometimes tough to find a lot of things you really don’t need. However, when you shift your focus to choosing what to keep, it is much easier to eliminate the things you do not need. You begin to identify the things that really matter to you. You soon realize that most of your other possessions don’t carry so much of an importance than those that are absolutely essential.

You are like a detective, looking for the culprits, but in this case, these are culprits that help you. You find the possessions that actually make you more productive or happy. For example, if you have a favorite coffee cup, you might not want to throw it away. But when you think of the actual cause, you might realize that you just enjoy a good cup of warm coffee in the morning, and the cup was just a means of enjoying it. The coffee beans are essential; the cup is not.

There is this thing called the 80-20 rule, which says that eighty percent of the results come from twenty percent of the causes. So identify the critical ‘twenty percent’ instead of trying to identify the other ‘eighty percent.’ Besides allowing you to single out the most vital possessions you own, it is also much more efficient to find the ‘twenty percent’ instead of the ‘eighty percent.’ It’s simple maths.

Instead of finding things to throw away, find things that you absolutely need to keep. You’ll end up getting rid of many more things this way.
