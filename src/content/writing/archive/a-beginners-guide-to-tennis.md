---
title: "a beginner’s guide to tennis"
date: 2022-09-03
---

Spinning dizzily, a fuzzy, neon-green ball shoots through the air like a star as a vague and woozy outline of a net passes beneath. It collides with the ground and bounces up again. A web of wire comes to meet in the air, making an agitated *whooshing*as it warps the air around it. They impact, sending shockwaves through the very core of the ball. The ball shoots in the other direction and it all starts over again. This is tennis, and rackets and balls and nets are the ingredients of this game.

The ball is the essence of tennis, and its composition is simple. It is slightly smaller than a baseball, and slightly softer too. It is fuzzy and green, like a wad of baby hair, dyed. In a swirly loop that covers the whole ball is a line of what looks like Wite-Out. Without it, tennis would be impossible to play. If you do not have rackets, you can use your hands. If you do not have a net, no problem. If you do not have a ball, what to do? 

The racket is the weapon with which players launch attacks on the opponent; its composition is rather complex. The part of the racket the player holds is like a hilt of a sword, and is wrapped up in what feels like leather. At the end of the grip, the single metal structure splits in two. The two parts extend until they meet at the top, forming an upside-down teardrop. Just above the point of diversion is an additional piece of curved metal that makes an oval along with the two diverging bits. In between this oval is tautly strung wire, which holds the whole thing together. With this elaborate structure, tennis players are able to hit balls at speeds of over one hundred miles per hour.

The final ingredient of this simple sport is the net. It appears as a blown up version of a ping pong net, with two metal poles supporting it at the ends. This net serves two functions. Firstly, it serves as a border between the territories of the two players. Secondly, it provides an additional thrill to what might have been a dull sport. It is an obstacle, and without it, tennis rallies would be short and repetitive. Thus, the net in the center is the final piece of the puzzle, that weaves the whole sport together into what it is.

Tennis is an eccentric sport. Two players wield dignified sticks stretched apart by webs of wire at the ends, use little green balls embellished with curvy white lines, and stare at each other across a vertically raised net. One player hits the ball a little too hard, a little too soft, or misses it altogether, and the crowd applauds, enraptured. Such is tennis.
