---
title: "How to Read More Books"
date: 2020-12-22
---

By applying a few simple strategies, you can substantially increase the amount you read. The strategies are (1) reading books you want to read, (2) reading every day, and (3) taking notes only inside the book.

## Read books you want to read

The amount of books you read is proportional to your desire to read them. When you sit down to read a book, be sure you wouldn’t rather read something else. If you would rather be reading something else, there is nothing stopping you from reading that book you actually want to read.

As you read more, you will find that you will begin to enjoy more complex books that you wouldn’t have appreciated in the past. So don’t start by reading the hardest books you can find. For example, if you were reading classics, it wouldn’t be a good idea to dive right into a work by Tolstoy. Instead, perhaps you would start by reading something by Twain or Hemingway. And as you read more classics, you would get to the point where you would actually appreciate the complexity involved in a work by Tolstoy. So start with easier books, and gradually build up to harder ones.

Sometimes when you read a book you don’t enjoy, you still feel obligated to finish it. But then, you would just be reading for the sake of finishing the book. There are more books out there than you could read in a lifetime; there is no reason you should be hung up on a certain book. So if you don’t enjoy certain books, don’t feel guilty to just move on.

## Read every day

To read more you must read consistently. So read every day. Block out a certain time of the day during which you will just read. Also read whenever you have spare time; be sure to have a book handy. By being consistent, your progress will begin to pile up and you will end up reading much more.

## Make your notes inside the book

If you want to make notes, I think it is better to take random notes inside the book. Taking organized notes in a notebook or on a computer while you are reading is almost multitasking. You have to continually shift your focus from just reading what the author is saying to writing down something you can reference later. You might even start spending more time taking notes than actually reading. So if you are reading a physical book, write your notes in the margin; if you are reading on a Kindle, use the annotation tool. These notes don’t have to be neat or clear; just give your thoughts somewhere to go. There is always time to make sense of them at a later time.
