---
title: "Leaving Books Unfinished"
date: 2021-05-29
---

There is this curious conundrum I used to find myself caught in. Often, with nothing better to do, I would find myself browsing the shelf in my room for some book I hadn’t yet read. Finding one, I would pluck it from its place among the other books and walk over to my desk. After sitting down, I would set the book on the desk with a flourish. The way I opened the book would make you think it was piping hot.

At first, my mind was clear and I was interested in what the book was all about, so I would stroll through the first few pages. My interest waned with every turn off the page and soon morphed into boredom. The book turned out to be a dud, but only after I had crossed the Rubicon. I didn’t want to read the book anymore and my mind felt as if ink had been spilt all over it. I would have dropped the book for something better, but I figured that dropping the book now would be throwing away time. Interest turned into boredom which turned into desperation; I would finish the book one way or another — the old man who moved the mountain would have been proud.

Wanting to end the suffering sooner, I read at a faster and faster pace until my eyes almost flew out of their sockets. I was floundering in an incoherent stream of words. Some might say I was speed reading, but I don’t think I was reading at all. I had been baited into the trap again.

A few months ago, when I had nothing to do and the city felt like a ghost town because nobody was going anywhere, I decided to analyze this little problem of mine. The answer I ended up with was two-fold. One, I decided that vanity prompted me to finish books I didn’t really care to read. After all, you sound well-read and educated if you can claim to have read fifty books in a year, including tomes like *Ulysses* or*Beyond Good and Evil *or something or other*.*

Vanity, however, was only part of the problem. After all, if all I wanted was to read more tomes, I still could have found more appealing ones. But the second problem came into play when I found myself caught partway through the book. I fell for the thought that if I stopped then, the time I had already spent reading would have been wasted. But in the end, I only wasted more time by finishing the book because with the way I ended up reading those books, God knows if I actually *read* them.
