## Say “hi” to the microbial you
All over your body, you have been colonized by microbes—bacteria, archaea, viruses, and others. Don’t worry, this is not a massive overthrow of your immune system. Instead, these microbes just want to maintain a symbiosis — with you. Trillions of these microbes (most of them living in your gut) and their genes make up what we call the human microbiome.¹ The microbes in your body exist in about equal numbers compared to your body cells, which means that for each human cell, you get a fellow microbe too.² All these microbes are spread across your body. But not every part of your microbiome is the same.³ Different parts of your bodies are like different environments: some places humid, some dry, etc.⁴ Microbiomes also vary between humans, as illustrated by the fact that some of us are more susceptible to mosquito bites.⁵ Together, these microbes contain about 500 times more genes than your own cells provide.⁴ We may conclude that your body cells cannot do as much as your microbes are most likely capable of. That is because microbes are an essential part of how your body functions and even have an effect on how you behave.

## Microbes and health
Nowadays, many people see microbes as things in need of destruction. From the antibiotics to the antibacterial wipes, it seems as if we are bent on killing microbes. But you must remember that *less than 1%* of microbes cause sickness.⁶ In Ed Yong’s book *I Contain Multitudes*, he advises us to see our immune system instead as an “immunostat.” Our immune system is a delicate balance between destruction and peace. When our immune systems are switched to “destroy”, we often cause self-inflicted harm, often attacking even our own cells. Turn it too low, and we become prone to infection. Our microbes partly contribute to this balance.⁷

> [Microbes] stimulate the production of pro-inflammatory cells, while others induce dove-like anti-inflammatory cells . . . they allow us to react to threats without overreacting . . . microbes craft and tune our immune system in the first place!

> Ed Yong, *I Contain Multitudes*

In short, microbes affect nearly every part of our body

## Microbes and how we behave
Many scientists believe that there is a microbiome-gut-brain axis, meaning that our brain and gut microbiome may have a bidirectional relationship. Past research has shown that our gut microbiota interact with our central nervous system⁸ (the brain and spinal cord), which suggests that our microbes may affect our behavior. Surveys showed that gastrointestinal symptoms were positively related to depression and anxiety, which supports the theory of a microbiome-gut-brain axis.⁹

Further evidence for the gut-microbiome-brain axis is made in a [recent study](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.humic.2019.100069) by an Oxford researcher. In this study, scientists found that sociability and neurotic behaviors were linked to the abundance of different types of microbes, meaning that the type of diversity in our microbiome can affect our behaviors. The study also showed that the composition of the microbiome was a predictor of anxiety and stress. People who were more stressed and anxious had low microbiome diversity while those who had a large social network had a greater microbiome diversity. However, it is also worth noting that the relationship between the gut microbiome and behavior goes both ways (bidirectional), meaning that behavior affects the microbiome, while the microbiome also affects behavior.¹⁰ (Here’s an analogy: writing more improves your writing, and improved writing makes you want to write more, or vice versa.) Through this study, the gut microbiome was associated with human behaviors, leading to the conclusion that we might be able to create probiotic or prebiotic treatments for people with behavioral disorders.

## Conclusion
We need to reevaluate how we treat microbes. There are many ways we can help them thrive. For example, diverse eaters and those who consumed *natural* probiotics were linked to a more diverse microbiome.¹⁰ Many of our modern diets also discourage the eating of fibers. Yet fibers turn out to be helpful in bringing in microbes that aid digestion.³ Most importantly, we must abandon the stigma associated with microbes. Most of them our friends, not enemies. Sure, harmful ones still exist, and for them, we must still rationally exercise caution. Confucius had it right when he called for moderation:

> The virtue embodied in the doctrine of the Mean is of the highest order.

> Analects, 6:29 (Burton Watson tr.)
