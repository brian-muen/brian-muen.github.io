When I was younger, I observed how different things fell and thought to myself, “It seems as if heavier objects fall faster than lighter ones.” I must have been around five years old then and hadn’t learned much about physics yet. The way feathers and snowflakes fall as if they’re trying to do it in the slowest way possible led to this idea.

But it wasn’t much later that I found out that everything I thought about the way things fall was wrong. I was told that heavier objects didn’t fall faster than lighter ones and it all had to do with air resistance.

A few years later and I still hadn’t quite figured everything out: what was the reason that some things fell faster? The question started to irk at me, like a coffee stain on a clean, white desk. It made me want to clean it all up. I would find a way to answer this question.

I fired up my computer and got to work. A little bit of research later, and I finally understood the actual reason some things fall faster than others. I packaged what I learned into this article.

Let us take a trip through history; let us explore the ideas of scientists who grappled with the very question that is before us.  And by the end of that journey, the reason some things fall faster than others will be clear.

## Aristotle

Aristotle was one of the earliest scientists who came up with a theory to explain how and why things fall. Aristotle was a Greek philosopher during the 4th century BC. To explain how and why things fall, Aristotle created an elaborate theory framed around the idea of natural place.

Building upon the work of his predecessors, Aristotle held the belief that there were four elements: earth, air, water, and fire. All matter was made of some combination of these elements.

Aristotle then theorized that each element was attracted to a certain area of the universe; these were called the natural place for each element. For example, earth (the heaviest element) had its natural place at the center of our planet while fire (the lightest) had its natural place located among the skies. This, Aristotle reasoned, was why smoke rose and raindrops fell.

Aristotle also had a theory for how things fall. He thought that the rate at which an object fell would be directly proportional to its weight. In other words, the heavier the object, the faster it fell; the lighter the object, the slower if fell.

Aristotle’s theory became widespread around the ancient world; it was the accepted explanation for why and how things fall. It was only two centuries later that better theories emerged to replace Aristotle’s theory. So let us fast forward two centuries into the age of the Renaissance.

## Galileo

The Renaissance: a period of scientific rigor unparalleled by past centuries. At the heart of this movement was a scientist by the name of Galileo Galilei. Galileo brought this new wave of scientific discovery into the realm of physics, particularly that of falling objects.

Galileo, unlike Aristotle, did not believe that the mass of an object would affect the rate at which it fell. Instead, he believed that, when there was no resistance, all objects would fall at the same rate.

Galileo’s theory of falling objects is made legendary by a popular tale. He is said to have gone to the top of the Tower of Pisa and dropped two balls of different weights over the edge. When both crashed to the ground at the same time, part of Aristotle’s theory crashed down along with them. 

## Newton

While Galileo dismantled part of Aristotle’s theory, it would soon be thoroughly obliterated.

About a century after Galileo’s time, another scientist continued where Galileo left off. This scientist was a man by the name of Isaac Newton.

Replacing Aristotle’s fanciful abstractions about elements and natural places, Newton proposed the existence of a force: gravity.¹ Gravity can be thought of as the force of attraction between any two objects in the universe.

Gravity is similar to the attraction between magnets of different poles.²

Just as the force of magnetism increases for larger magnets, the force of gravity increases for objects of larger mass. Distance also affects the amount of force. The magnetic force between magnets increases as the area between them decreases. The same happens with the gravitational force between two objects.³

Isaac Newton was able to sum up the effects of gravity in this little equation: F = G(m₁m₂/d²). F represents the force of gravity; G is the gravitational constant which means it always stays the same; m₁ and m₂ are the two objects; and d is the distance between the two objects.

Newton’s law of universal gravitation describes the force of gravity between two objects, but with it alone, we have no way of knowing how fast an object will fall. We must find a way to insert some measure of speed or acceleration into our equation.

For this, we turn to Newton’s second law of motion, which relates the acceleration of an object to force and mass. The second law of motion is represented by this equation: a = F/m. If we rearrange this equation a bit, we get this: F = ma.

When we calculate this acceleration, the mass of the object cancels out from both sides of the equation. This means that the mass of the object does not affect the rate at which it falls: exactly what Galileo figured out. Galileo also noted that this would only happen if there was no resistance present. The key to our answer, then, lies in finding out what this mysterious resistance is.

## Air resistance

Friction is the force that goes against the motion of any object. You see, when gravity pulls an object down, it is not the only force that acts upon the object. Friction presents itself in the form of air resistance.

An example of air resistance can be found in the activity of skydiving. By deploying a parachute, you are able to slow down the rate at which you fall. It follows that deploying the parachute increased the air resistance by changing the shape of your mass. Turns out that this mysterious resistance is air resistance.

We have finally come to our conclusion: what actually affects how fast an object falls is not a matter of mass, but that of air resistance.
