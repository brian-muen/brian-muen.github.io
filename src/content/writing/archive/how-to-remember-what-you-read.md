---
title: "How to Remember What You Read Without Taking Notes"
date: 2020-10-12
---

When we read nonfiction books, we often want to remember more of what we read. Somehow, we want to know that we learned something from the book and the time we spent on it was worth it. 

There are many people out there who suggest taking notes to remember more of what you read. Somehow, taking notes never seems to work for me. Because, sometimes, we just want to read without doing anything else.  

## Read to Understand

When you are reading nonfiction books, you really need to understand the concepts. The objective is not to remember random minutiae, but to get a broader understanding of the concepts. For example:

If you were reading about special relativity, it would be more important to understand how time dilation works instead of memorizing the exact speed of light of 299,792,458 m/s (I looked that up). After all, the man who developed the theory of special relativity, Albert Einstein, once said, “Never memorize something that you can look up.”

If you try to memorize everything, you will eventually forget it anyway if you never get to apply it. But if you understand the concepts, it will be much easier for you to memorize additional information when it is necessary for you to do so. 

And, let’s be honest, it might seem cool that you can recite the speed of light from memory, but if you cannot actually apply it to physics, it probably isn’t useful realistically. You need to know how the details fit into the big picture. If you don’t get the big picture, you’re like somebody trying to memorize everyone’s phone numbers but not knowing how to use the phone.

## Read more

> Read 500 pages like this every day. That’s how knowledge works. It builds up, like compound interest. All of you can do it, but I guarantee not many of you will do it.— Warren Buffet

In [an interview with Quartz](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8xwh88cI_d8), Bill Gates mentioned that ideas are easier to understand when you read more because you start to see similarities between different ideas. For example:

- I read *Goodbye Things* by Fumio Sasaki. In the book, he introduced the idea of minimalism and how we should be content with what we have. Later, I read *The Manual* by Epictetus. Epictetus talked about how we should not desire for things that are not within our control. I soon saw similarities between what Sasaki said about being content with what we have and Epictetus’ idea of not desiring things without are control. In a way, their ideas overlapped. If you are content with what you have, you will not desire things that are outside your control; and if you do not desire things outside your control, you will be content with what you have.

I also think that it also helps to see the same idea from different perspectives, because one author may present something in a different way than another author may. By exposing yourself to a topic from many unique perspectives, the puzzle pieces will begin to click and you will have a broader understanding of the topic.

## Consult other sources

Also, you don’t have to limit yourself to just taking information from other books. For example, I came across the key ideas of DNA translation, transcription, and replication in Dr. Siddhartha Mukherjee’s book *The Gene*. The book was mainly a history of genetics, so it did not delve much into the underlying processes. It was only after I watched Khan Academy, Amoeba Sisters, and a few Nature articles did the ideas really start to click. But by having already read *The Gene*, I learned the history behind the science of genetics, making these concepts more meaningful instead of becoming things I was forced to memorize.

By reading The Gene, I could look at the double helix and remember the story of Watson and Crick and Franklin. I could learn about the genome and recall the Human Genome Project headed by Dr. Francis Collins. I could even look at a pea plant and remember Mendel’s discoveries. By reading more books, we can, in a way, form new perspectives of the world around us.
