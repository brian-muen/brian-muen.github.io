---
title: "Lessons in Minimalism"
date: 2021-11-08
---

There is this shelf in the corner of my living room, beside a round glass table where you can’t see the glass because of the souvenirs. On the bottom level of the shelf is a coffee-brown box made of a rough fabric that might be burlap. And in this box is a random collection of books that I haven’t bothered to read.

It was summer in the year two thousand twenty and I was almost as bored as the hour hand of a grandfather clock. I sat on a stool in the kitchen and contemplated the strangeness of life. But I’m not a philosopher so I said *why not*and went over to the shelf in the corner of the living room because I had nothing better to do. After a few sprained wrists I managed to heave the box off the shelf. Nobody had been in there since who knows when and all the books were comfy in a blanket of dust. After some swiping and some coughing a cover peeked out at me. The cover image was of a plain blue teacup on a plain wood shelf, and the text read *goodbye, things.*I drew the book out and flipped through the pages. A couple of hours later and I had gotten a whiff of what something called minimalism was all about.

Since reading the book, I have uncluttered my room by throwing away most of everything. I also compiled a list of random observations from minimalism that might be useful.

1: Tidying up is a waste of time if you don’t throw anything away. Everything will become a mess again and you will have to tidy up again. You’re better off not tidying in the first place because soon you will be entangled in a perpetual cycle of tidying that spirals towards nowhere and is never full of itself. You are already in the underworld with Sisyphus, doing something only to do it again and again forever. But if you get rid of enough things, you will have nothing to tidy. Problem solved.

2: Choose what to keep, not what to throw away. Trying to find something to throw away is a great way to end up where you began. You’ll always find an excuse to keep that spare button from that shirt you don’t even own anymore or that other shirt you haven’t even worn before. But if you instead try to find things you need to keep, it will make everything go much more smoothly because you probably won’t end up finding much of anything at all.

It’s also much more efficient this way. Why anyone would try to find the many things to throw away instead of trying to find the few things to keep is beyond my math skills.

3: More is less. When you have more you have so much that your life is infested by things that don’t do you any good. Why, again, did you spend your life’s savings on a shirt with an ugly red sticker on it, and why is it still in your closet? What is that post-it note from the last century doing on your refrigerator? You waste your eyes whenever you look around. You waste your life spending time with things that don’t need to be there. You waste your life cleaning a house that’s cluttered with nothingness. Less is more.

4: Aspirations are meaningless if you’re not already content. Aspirations keep you moving in life, but if you’re not already content, you will be continually pursuing what you don’t have, and there’s always something you don’t have. When I was little, I read a story about a boy who hoodwinked his donkey. He took a stick and stuck an ear of corn onto the end. The boy then held the stick in front of the donkey so that it would go wherever he wanted it to. If you have aspirations without being content in the first place, you are just like that donkey . . . except that you are the one hoodwinking yourself.

5: *A and B sauntered into a cafe. Jazz played from somewhere, as smooth as whipped cream on a melting chocolate chip cookie. Dim lights glowed from above because the owner had to pay rent. They found an empty oak table and sat down across from each other. The chairs were dignified tree stumps because the owner really had to pay rent. A barista came and took their orders: a cappuccino and a latte. The barista scurried away.*

*“It’s a big day today,” said A, trying to settle into the tree stump.*

*“Yeah?”*

*“My birthday, don’t you remember? Today’s gonna be brilliant.”*

*“Because it’s your birthday?”*

*“Yup.”*

*B thought that A’s logic was a bit wobbly.*

Happiness is a choice. You shouldn’t be happy on certain holidays, at least not any more than you normally are. Holidays are arbitrary dates in an arbitrary thing called a year. Almost everything about most holidays is arbitrary. Don’t be a marionette twisted around by what your calendar says. Make happiness the marionette.
